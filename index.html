Introduction

On December 30th, 2019, the first cases of COVID-19 were reported in China by doctor in Wuhan. That doctor, Li Wenliang, tried to warn the world of the impending catastrophe, but was silenced by his authoritarian government. Two months later, he and thousands of others had succumbed to the virus. As COVID-19 spread, the world slowly realized it's threat, and went on full lockdown. Ports and borders were closed, workers were asked to stay home, and much of the global economy was shuttered. In the months following the outbreak of COVID-19, much has been said on how it will affect the economy. This analysis will invert this trend and look at if the size of a country's economy impacts the spread of COVID-19. This analysis looks at both Infection and Mortality Rates for countries around the world, comparing these to each country's GDP. Use the links above for an in-depth look at both variables.

Discussion

These visuals analyze available GDP, Infection Rate, and Mortality Rate data to determine whether there is a correlation between the size of a country's economy and it's overall reaction to COVID-19. I chose to analyze this data to determine whether or not countries that were more active in the global economy were hit harder by COVID-19. Both the bar charts and the scatterplots show that there is no significant relationship between GDP, Infection Rate, and Mortality Rate, at least with currently available data.

Limitations

There are many interesting limitations on this data, and many more dynamics that will change the scope and outcome of this analysis in the future. As scientists collect more data, they are realizing that many more people than previously suspected have already been exposed to COVID-19. This means that the data for cases is a low-end estimate of the actual number of cases. In reality, due to a pervasive lack of testing in many countries, including the United States, we will never know the actual Infection or even Mortality Rates. Moreover, the world is witnessing political falsification of data for multiple reasons, including saving face for the incumbent regime. China is strongly suspected of underreporting cases and deaths from the virus. In terms of the specific datasets that were used in this analysis, there are also important limitations. For example, many countries do not have available GDP data for the 2018 and 2019 fiscal years. Accordingly, this analysis uses GDP data from 2017.

Conclusion

In conclusion, this project attempts to analyze a potential relationship between GDP and COVID-19 Infection and Mortality Rates. The attempt was greatly hindered by numerous limitations on the data, including lack of testing, political corruption, and failure to report current GDP data. As a result, it is difficult to draw accurate conclusions at this time. What we can say is that the data available shows no correlation between GDP, Infection Rate, and Mortality Rate. This conclusion could change dramatically as better data becomes available. In terms of this analysis and the world, the future is uncertain.
