Comparing Mortality Rate to GDP

In this section, a country's GDP is compared to it's Mortality Rate. GDP is recorded as annual data from 2017, and Mortality Rate is the number of deaths which have occurred in a country divided by that country's total population.

Bar Chart

The following bar chart illustrates mortality rates for countries around the world. It is ordered by each country's GDP, starting from lowest on the left, to highest on the right. If there were a relationship between GDP and Coronavirus Mortality Rate, then countries with similar Mortality Rate would be grouped together. As shown, this is not the case. Countries with vastly different GDPs, like Russia and Antigua, can have similar infection rates while countries with similar GDPs like Indonesia and Bangladesh can have vastly different Mortality Rates. Similar to the Infection Rate data, there seems to be a slight decrease in the frequency of relatively high Mortality Rates as countries GDPs increase. Again, however, this trend is too weak to warrant further analysis, as other variables, such as medical technology, are likely better indicators. Again, it is worth noting that some countries have been ommitted from this visualization even if their data was available, since they were large outliers. The lack of uniformity and abundance of outliers further strengthens the argument that there is no significant relationship between a country's GDP and Mortality Rate.

ScatterPlot

This scatterplot directly visualizes the Mortality Rate compared to GDP for countries around the world. To view the data for each point, hover your cursor over the point of interest. The Mortality Rate for each country has also been multiplied to show data in a more reasonable scale. Since each countries GDP can vary by multiple orders of magnitude, the horizontal (x) axis is scaled logarithmically. In this case, the scatterplot truly lives up to its name, as the data seems to be randomly scattered across the plot. This, again, tells us that there is no statistically significant relationship between Mortality Rate and GDP. Countries with similar GDP like Ethiopia and Morocco have differing infection rates, and counties with starkly different GDP like Indonesia and Liberia can have similar Mortality Rates. Again, the seemingly random cluster of points gives more creedence to the argument that there is no significant relationship between a country's GDP and their Mortality Rate.
